# Linear Regression: A Step-by-Step Mathematical Walkthrough

This document provides a detailed, calculation-oriented explanation of simple linear regression. We'll work through a concrete example and show every mathematical step, using proper statistical notation.

---

## 1. What is Linear Regression?

Linear regression models the relationship between:

- **Independent variable (predictor/feature)**: denoted as \( X \)
- **Dependent variable (response/target)**: denoted as \( Y \)

The goal is to find a straight line that best describes this relationship, allowing us to **predict** \( Y \) for new values of \( X \).

The linear equation is:

\[
\hat{y}_i = b_0 + b_1 x_i
\]

where:
- \(\hat{y}_i\) = predicted value for the \(i\)-th observation
- \(x_i\) = observed predictor value
- \(b_1\) = slope (change in predicted \(Y\) per unit change in \(X\))
- \(b_0\) = intercept (predicted \(Y\) when \(X = 0\))

The **"best" line** minimizes the sum of squared vertical distances between the observed data points and the line – this is the **ordinary least squares (OLS)** criterion.

---

## 2. Example Dataset

We use a simple real‑world scenario: predicting exam score from hours studied.

| Hours Studied (\(x_i\)) | Exam Score (\(y_i\)) |
|:-----------------------:|:--------------------:|
|            1            |          50          |
|            2            |          55          |
|            3            |          65          |
|            4            |          70          |
|            5            |          80          |

Number of observations: \( n = 5 \).

---

## 3. Step 1: Compute the Means

The mean of \(X\) and \(Y\) are needed for the slope and intercept formulas.

\[
\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i = \frac{1+2+3+4+5}{5} = \frac{15}{5} = 3.0
\]

\[
\bar{y} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i = \frac{50+55+65+70+80}{5} = \frac{320}{5} = 64.0
\]

---

## 4. Step 2: Recall the OLS Formulas

**Slope** (\(b_1\)):

\[
b_1 = \frac{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}) }{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2 }
\]

**Intercept** (\(b_0\)):

\[
b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1 \bar{x}
\]

These formulas come from minimizing the sum of squared residuals.

---

## 5. Step 3: Build a Computation Table

We calculate each term systematically.

| \(i\) | \(x_i\) | \(y_i\) | \(x_i - \bar{x}\) | \(y_i - \bar{y}\) | \((x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})\) | \((x_i - \bar{x})^2\) |
|:----:|:------:|:------:|:-----------------:|:-----------------:|:---------------------------------:|:---------------------:|
| 1    | 1      | 50     | 1 - 3 = **-2**    | 50 - 64 = **-14** | \((-2)(-14) = 28\)                | \((-2)^2 = 4\)        |
| 2    | 2      | 55     | 2 - 3 = **-1**    | 55 - 64 = **-9**  | \((-1)(-9) = 9\)                  | \((-1)^2 = 1\)        |
| 3    | 3      | 65     | 3 - 3 = **0**     | 65 - 64 = **1**   | \(0 \times 1 = 0\)                 | \(0^2 = 0\)           |
| 4    | 4      | 70     | 4 - 3 = **1**     | 70 - 64 = **6**   | \(1 \times 6 = 6\)                 | \(1^2 = 1\)           |
| 5    | 5      | 80     | 5 - 3 = **2**     | 80 - 64 = **16**  | \(2 \times 16 = 32\)               | \(2^2 = 4\)           |
| **Sum** | | | | | \(\mathbf{75}\) | \(\mathbf{10}\) |

---

## 6. Step 4: Calculate the Slope (\(b_1\))

\[
b_1 = \frac{ \sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}) }{ \sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2 } = \frac{75}{10} = 7.5
\]

Thus, for each additional hour studied, the predicted exam score increases by **7.5 points**.

---

## 7. Step 5: Calculate the Intercept (\(b_0\))

\[
b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1 \bar{x} = 64.0 - (7.5 \times 3.0) = 64.0 - 22.5 = 41.5
\]

The intercept indicates that a student who studies **0 hours** would be predicted to score **41.5**. (In many contexts the intercept may not be interpretable, but it is mathematically necessary.)

---

## 8. Step 6: Write the Final Regression Equation

\[
\hat{y}_i = 41.5 + 7.5\,x_i
\]

For the given data, the predicted values \(\hat{y}_i\) are:

| \(x_i\) | \(y_i\) (actual) | \(\hat{y}_i = 41.5 + 7.5x_i\) |
|:------:|:----------------:|:----------------------------:|
| 1      | 50               | \(41.5 + 7.5(1) = 49.0\)     |
| 2      | 55               | \(41.5 + 7.5(2) = 56.5\)     |
| 3      | 65               | \(41.5 + 7.5(3) = 64.0\)     |
| 4      | 70               | \(41.5 + 7.5(4) = 71.5\)     |
| 5      | 80               | \(41.5 + 7.5(5) = 79.0\)     |

Notice that the predicted values lie exactly on the line, and the differences (residuals) \(y_i - \hat{y}_i\) are:  
\(1.0,\; -1.5,\; 1.0,\; -1.5,\; 1.0\). Their sum is zero (as expected).

---

## 9. Step 7: Use the Model for Prediction

**Question**: What exam score would we predict for a student who studies **6 hours**?

\[
\hat{y} = 41.5 + 7.5 \times 6 = 41.5 + 45.0 = 86.5
\]

So the predicted score is **86.5**.

## 11. Key Mathematical Insights

- The **slope** formula is derived from the covariance of \(X\) and \(Y\) divided by the variance of \(X\):
  \[
  b_1 = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\text{Var}(X)}
  \]
- The **intercept** ensures that the line passes through the point of means \((\bar{x}, \bar{y})\).
- The **least squares** criterion guarantees that the sum of squared residuals is minimized:
  \[
  \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2 \to \min
  \]
- In our example, the total sum of squares (\( \sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2 \)) is:
  \[
  (-14)^2 + (-9)^2 + (1)^2 + (6)^2 + (16)^2 = 196 + 81 + 1 + 36 + 256 = 570
  \]
  The regression sum of squares (\( \sum (\hat{y}_i - \bar{y})^2 \)) is:
  \[
  (49-64)^2 + (56.5-64)^2 + (64-64)^2 + (71.5-64)^2 + (79-64)^2 = 225 + 56.25 + 0 + 56.25 + 225 = 562.5
  \]
  and the residual sum of squares is \(570 - 562.5 = 7.5\). The coefficient of determination \(R^2 = 562.5 / 570 \approx 0.987\), indicating an excellent fit.

---

## 12. Why Linear Regression Matters

Linear regression is a foundational tool in statistics and machine learning. It is used for:

- **Prediction** (sales, prices, demand)
- **Inference** (understanding relationships between variables)
- **Trend analysis** (time series, economics)
- **Risk modeling** (finance, insurance)

Its simplicity, interpretability, and strong theoretical basis make it a first‑choice method in many data analysis tasks.

---

## 13. Summary of Steps

1. Collect paired data \((x_i, y_i)\).
2. Compute the means \(\bar{x}\) and \(\bar{y}\).
3. Calculate the deviations \(x_i - \bar{x}\) and \(y_i - \bar{y}\).
4. Compute the sum of products \(\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})\) and the sum of squares \(\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2\).
5. Obtain the slope \(b_1 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}\).
6. Obtain the intercept \(b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1\bar{x}\).
7. Form the regression equation \(\hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x\).
8. Use the equation to predict \(Y\) for new \(X\) values.

This step‑by‑step procedure embodies the core idea of linear regression: finding the line that best fits the data by minimizing squared errors.
